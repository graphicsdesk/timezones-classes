[You want to add COVID-19 to headline to give relevancy + SEO keyword] [As COVID-19 carries into the new semester, Columbia does little to help struggling international students learn across different time zones.]

By Jun Yi Zhang, Melissa Wang, and Michelle Xu Edited by Jessica Li Illustration by Brenda Huang

March XX, 2021

When the COVID-19 pandemic sent home Columbia’s undergraduate students, late nights, social isolation, and general exhaustion became the norm for a particular group of international students. These students come from China, Hong Kong, South Korea, Japan, and India—each consistently ranked within the top 10 passports that comprise Columbia’s 18 percent international undergraduate population. They returned to time zones that are on average 12 hours ahead of New York’s Eastern Standard Time zone.

[impossible to accommodate all time zones]

With remote students scattered across more than 115 countries and a wide variation of time zones, there is very little that the University can do to schedule classes in a way that accommodates all students, especially for large classes with few sections. International students recognize and understand the difficulties in accommodating all time zones; “there’s a limit to what they [the University] can do,” Whitney Deng, a Barnard first-year currently studying from Hong Kong noted.

[visa & travel issues]

Returning to campus for the Fall 2020 semester was not an option for many of these students. On July 6, 2020, the Student and Exchange Visitor Program under the Trump administration banned international students from entering or remaining in the United States if they had a “fully online course load.” Although Trump revoked these exemptions eight days later following pressure from numerous external sources, international students continued to face barriers from entering the U.S.

While continuing students with fully online course loads were able to remain in the United States, nonimmigrant students in “New” or “Initial” status were still restricted from entering the country with a fully online course schedule for fall 2020.

The suspension of non-immigrant visa services in many embassies and consulates during the pandemic also meant that new international students and continuing students who needed visa renewals were unable to proceed with their visa application processes. Some embassies have slowly resumed non-immigrant services. However only those in Korea and the Philippines did so before Columbia’s fall semester began. Services in China remain suspended as of publication, andthe Department of State is still unable to provide a specific date as to when each embassy will resume all services.

There is an extra challenge for those who possess functioning student Visas in China: Presidential proclamations have suspended entry into the United States of noncitizens who have been to China anytime during the 14-days prior to their attempted entry. As such, Chinese international students must stop by a third country for at least 14 days before they are allowed into the United States.

[quick thing about physical repercussions]

Besides the myriad of travel restrictions international students face into the present, these students must also continue to cope with a disrupted circadian rhythms. With these changes come a host of potential health issues. StudiesAs multiple pieces of research have shown that alterations in daily routine, such as modifying one’s sleep schedule in order to accommodate a time difference, can harm one’s physical and mental health. A broken sleep pattern can result in poor study efficiency as well as negative mood changes and fatigue.

[schools’ effort to help]

As the pandemic continued, Barnard and Columbia’s Core Office made efforts to help international students attend their classes. At the end of the fall 2020 semester, Barnard surveyed its students on which classes they intended to take in the upcoming spring semester and how important those classes were to them. Because the survey was released with the aim of providing feedback to Barnard when planning for the spring 2021 semester, many international students expected the college to accommodate their living in different time zones. However, for Katya Reichert, a Barnard first-year who spent the fall semester studying from the Philippines, the scheduled times of the top two courses that she indicated—Introduction to Art History II and Data Structures in Java—remained the same. “I don’t know if [the survey] was performative, or if not enough people chose the classes that I chose,” Reichert said.

Meanwhile, international students taking mandatory core classes also saw fruitless outcomes in the Core office’s effort to accommodate their time zones. In August, the Center for the Core Curriculum gave all students who were enrolled in Contemporary Civilization, Art Humanities, and/or Music Humanities the opportunity to petition for a new section if their current section met between 10 p.m. and 7 a.m. in their local time zone. However, when Columbia College sophomore Kevin Sun Park requested to switch out of his Contemporary Civilization section that was scheduled for 4 a.m. in Seoul, his petition was rejected by the Core Office.

“I never doubted that I wouldn’t be able to switch to another section,” explained Park. “Because I would have been taking classes from 4 a.m. to 6 a.m. In what kind of world would a university force me to do classes at that time?”

The School of Engineering and School of General Studies have not distributed surveys nor offered petition options.

[shit course offerings]

scrollytelling

For Kevin, who resides in GMT+9, the distribution of mandatory Columbia College underclassmen seminars (Literature Humanities, Frontiers of Science, University Writing, and Contemporary Civilization) over the day saw little change from the pre-COVID spring 2020 semester to post-COVID semesters.

While morning classes that end around noon EST and midnight GMT+9 are friendly to both American and many international students, the college has provided very few classes in that time period.

Compared to the pre-COVID spring semester, Columbia College added only five Core seminar sections that ended between 11 a.m. and 11 p.m. GMT+9 time to the spring 2021 semester.

The distribution of major requirement courses is even more unfriendly to the majority of international students than that of mandatory Core seminars. The number of major requirement classes ending at a particular hour peaks earlier in the night, between 2 a.m. and 3 a.m.

The distribution of all courses mirrors the distribution of major requirement classes.

[recs for asynchronous teaching across time zones]

Columbia’s Center for Teaching and Learning published a guideline for asynchronous teaching across time zones. The guide provides tips such as setting due dates that take into account time zone differences, “[e]ncourag[ing] students to adjust the time zone in CourseWorks (Canvas) so they do not miss due dates,” and placing students in similar time zones together for group projects. It also asks that instructors “be as responsive as possible” especially to students who miss out on live conversations given time zones differences.

Professors have taken their own initiatives to ease the difficulty of learning in classes at unnatural hours, such as allowing asynchronous attendance, offering international-friendly office hours, and scheduling alternative exam times.

However, [the University still does not require professors to allow asynchronous attendance, meaning some students are still required to make the choice between academics, health, relationships, and extracurricular interests.]

[conclusion (nodding towards summer 2021 course offerings)]

Considering President Bollinger’s announcement of a three-semester plan, Fall 2020 and Spring 2021 is no longer representative of the full course offerings for the 2020-21 school year. As of publication, Summer 2021 course offerings have not been updated for all departments. It is still unknown if the summer semester will be a chance for international students to have a healthier and more inclusive academic option amidst an ongoing pandemic.
